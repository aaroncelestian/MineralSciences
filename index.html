About

The central question driving my research program is not compositional — what minerals are — but functional: what minerals do, and under what conditions their behavior becomes consequential for living systems, planetary processes, or both. Answering that requires going to where the chemistry happens — and, just as often, having the right collaborators bring it back to the lab.

As Curator of Mineral Sciences at the Natural History Museum of Los Angeles County, my research spans some of the more chemically extreme environments on Earth. Hypersaline lake beds in Western Australia preserve microbial communities operating under geochemical conditions analogous to early Mars — a natural laboratory for biosignature research that my collaborators and I access through both fieldwork and curated sample exchange. Geothermal brines in California are proving productive for understanding how salt-tolerant bacteria mediate lithium mobility at the mineral-fluid interface, work that draws on samples collected across a network of field partners. And the crystalline architecture of human kidney stones, it turns out, is far more biologically structured than the clinical literature had recognized — a finding that emerged not from the field at all, but from material that arrived through clinical and research collaborators. Each of these settings contributes specimens and data subsequently analyzed using the museum's full research infrastructure: X-ray diffraction, Raman spectroscopy, and high-resolution electron imaging, among other methods.

Field Exploration

Field work in mineral sciences is not incidental to the research — it is where the questions get set. The specimens, the geochemical context, the sequence in which minerals formed relative to one another: none of that is recoverable from a sample that arrives without its field data. Research

Every research program I pursue follows the same thread: minerals record. A crystal grown in a hypersaline lake records the chemistry of the water that made it — and whether anything was alive in that water. A kidney stone records the bacteria that helped build it. A microporous titanium silicate records a geometry that evolution independently discovered in cell membranes. My work is the act of reading those records — and deciding what to do with what they say.

Mineral Discovery

Finding a new mineral is the beginning, not the end. Each new species is a new arrangement of atoms — and asking what it can do is a far harder problem than finding it.

Each new mineral requires rigorous crystallographic characterization — single-crystal X-ray diffraction, electron microprobe analysis, Raman spectroscopy — before the International Mineralogical Association will approve it as a genuine addition to the geological record. That process is demanding. But it is straightforward compared to what comes next.

The real work is asking what the mineral does. Translating a new crystal geometry into something useful requires mineralogy, chemistry, physics, biology, and computational modeling of atomic structure. It requires experimental design across multiple disciplines and years of iterative testing. Most mineral discoverers stop at the name. This laboratory doesn't.

Rowleyite — discovered here — is currently under investigation as a selective chemotherapy delivery agent. The same structural properties that make it crystallographically distinctive may make it capable of targeting tumor cells with a precision that conventional delivery methods cannot achieve. A mineral named for a mine in the Arizona desert, potentially deployed in an oncology ward. That distance is the distance this research program is designed to travel.

Publications

A single crystal geometry — discovered here — now entombs nuclear waste and saves lives in emergency rooms.

The mechanism responsible for extraordinary Cs ion selectivity in crystalline silicotitanate

Journal of the American Chemical Society, 130(35), 11689–11694 · 2008 · doi.org/10.1021/ja801134a

This paper described the double lever mechanism — a coupled two-site ion exchange geometry in crystalline silicotitanate that explains its extraordinary selectivity for cesium ions. The same principle underlies both the remediation of radioactive wastewater from Fukushima Daiichi and Lokelma, an FDA-approved drug for life-threatening hyperkalemia. One mechanism. Two completely different applications. Both saving lives.

Bacteria don't just sit on kidney stones — they're built into them. This changes everything about how we treat the disease.

Intercalated bacterial biofilms are intrinsic internal components of calcium-based kidney stones

Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 123(5), e2517066123 · 2026 · doi.org/10.1073/pnas.2517066123

Using high-resolution electron imaging and synchrotron XRD, this study demonstrated that bacterial biofilms are structurally woven into the calcium oxalate matrix of kidney stones — not surface contamination but intrinsic architectural components. The finding reframes kidney stone disease as partly microbial in origin, opening direct pathways to antibiotic and microbiome-targeted therapies for a condition affecting 1 in 11 people worldwide.

The structural foundation — understanding how iron minerals form at the nanoscale, with implications for environmental remediation and planetary chemistry.

Similarities in 2- and 6-line ferrihydrite based on pair distribution function analysis of X-ray total scattering

Chemistry of Materials, 19(6), 1489–1496 · 2007 · doi.org/10.1021/cm062585n

Ferrihydrite is one of the most common iron minerals on Earth and Mars — yet its structure was poorly understood. This paper resolved a long-standing debate using total X-ray scattering and pair distribution function analysis, establishing the structural relationship between its two forms. It remains one of the most-cited papers in environmental mineralogy.

A NASA-commissioned roadmap for detecting life on other worlds — through minerals.

The Biology of Biosignature Detection

Keck Institute for Space Studies, Caltech · 2024 · Full report PDF

Commissioned by the Keck Institute for Space Studies at Caltech, this report synthesized the state of biosignature science for planetary exploration — what chemical and mineralogical signatures life leaves behind, how they survive geological time, and how future Mars missions should be designed to detect them. It represents the direct connection between laboratory mineral science and the search for life beyond Earth.

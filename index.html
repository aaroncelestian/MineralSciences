About

I am a researcher focused on the ways minerals interact with their environments and living systems, and how these interactions can be harnessed to address global challenges such as climate change, pollution, and disease. My work lies at the intersection of science, art, and culture, with a particular interest in rethinking how we relate to and understand our planet.

As Curator of Mineral Sciences at the Natural History Museum of Los Angeles County, I employ advanced analytical techniques including X-ray diffraction, Raman spectroscopy, and high-resolution imaging to investigate mineral-environment interactions. My research spans from searching for biosignatures in salt crystals to developing sustainable lithium extraction methods.

Research Interests: Astrobiology • Environmental Remediation • Energy Materials • Cultural Heritage • PharmaMineralogy

Research Projects

Searching for Life in Salt Crystals

Collaboration: UCLA PERL Group, USC | Funding: NASA

Planetary exploration of Mars and the icy moons of Jupiter and Saturn have the potential to harbor life. My research focuses on finding and characterizing biological signatures in minerals such as halite and epsomite (among other common salts) to explore ways of finding life on other planets. On Earth, hypersaline bacteria are trapped in fluid inclusions of salt crystals as they grow and can be preserved through deep time. By investigating how to best analyze these minerals and identify bacteria without opening their crystal tomb, we can determine the bacteria-mineral relationship that allows these creatures to persist for millions of years.

Sustainable Lithium Extraction from Brines

Funding: Department of Energy | Status: Ongoing

Lithium is a promising metal with many applications in the energy industry. However, its extraction is not without environmental problems. The Department of Energy funds me to help develop and characterize new ways of extracting lithium from brine water at geothermal power plants, wastewater at oil field sites, and potentially water from the ocean during desalination. The lithium removal is simple and doesn't require any input to work, making it a low-energy/low-cost solution to global lithium demand and environmental security. Lithium manganese oxide (LMO) has great potential to provide economically viable Li extraction from a variety of natural brines with adequate capacity, selectivity, and loading/unloading kinetics.

Environmental Detoxification & Remediation

Collaborations: La Brea Tar Pits, Prospering Backyards Project

My research explores processes that use minerals to remove harmful substances from the environment. At the La Brea Tar Pits, I'm developing ways to use minerals that take energy from the Sun to break down asphalt into light organic compounds that can be used to make carbon fiber—a near-carbon-neutral process. Through the Prospering Backyards Pacific Standard Time Art Exhibit, I collaborate with community scientists, artists, and scientists to research how minerals could be used for reducing lead exposure in contaminated backyards. These same minerals can also absorb rare earth elements from waste streams and from the human body (e.g., gadolinium used for MRI contrast).

Kidney Stone Formation & Disease

Status: Ongoing

Understanding the microbiological factors contributing to calcium kidney stone formation is vital for prevention. While it is well known that urease-producing bacteria contribute to struvite stone growth, it is less clear how bacteria may contribute to the development of other types of stones. Bacteria such as E. coli and P. aeruginosa have been detected in patient-derived calcium oxalate stones even in the absence of infection. Our study aims to elucidate the impact of bacteria on calcium oxalate stone formation through high-resolution imaging of de novo calcium oxalate crystals in the presence of bacteria.

Art & Cultural Heritage: Getty Museum Cabinet Analysis

Collaboration: Getty Museum

The Getty Museum recently acquired the Borghese-Windsor Cabinet, a piece of furniture extensively decorated with agate, lapis lazuli, and other stones built around 1620 for Camillo Borghese (later Pope Paul V). A primary research goal was to determine if the agates in the cabinet are of a single origin or have more than one geologic provenance. It was traditionally thought that all agate gemstones acquired during the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries were sourced from the Nahe River Valley near Idar-Oberstein, Germany. Using near-infrared imaging to map molecular water inside the agate gems, we discovered that the gems were sourced from different mines, revealing secrets about Renaissance-era gem trade routes.
